Methods of Data Collection

What is Data Collection?

Data collection is the systematic process of gathering information (data) from relevant sources to answer questions, test hypotheses, or analyze trends.

üëâ In simple words:

Data collection is how we get the raw facts before doing statistics.

Why is it Important?

üìò Statistics

  • Foundation All statistical analysis starts with data
  • Quality Wrong data ‚Üí wrong conclusions
  • Accuracy Quality of data collection determines reliability

üíª Data Science & AI/ML

  • Learning Models learn only from collected data
  • Bias Poor data collection leads to biased models
  • Success Real-world AI success = good data + good models

üíº Business & Research

  • Research Market research and customer behavior analysis
  • Decisions Policy decisions and strategic planning
  • Methods Used in surveys, experiments, and case studies

üîë Key Terms

Data Raw facts or observations (numbers, text, images, etc.)
Population Entire group under study (Example: All students in a university)
Sample A subset of the population (Example: 100 students selected)
Primary Data Data collected first-hand by the researcher
Secondary Data Data collected from existing sources

Classification of Data Collection Methods

Broadly, there are two main methods:

üü¢ Primary Data Collection Methods
(Data collected by the researcher directly)
üîµ Secondary Data Collection Methods
(Data already collected by someone else)

Primary Data Collection Methods

1Ô∏è‚É£ Direct Personal Investigation

  • The investigator personally visits respondents and collects data
  • Questions are asked directly and answers are recorded by the investigator
  • Most accurate and reliable, but time-consuming and costly
Example

A researcher personally interviewing farmers about crop yield.

2Ô∏è‚É£ Indirect Oral Investigation

  • Data are collected from third persons or witnesses instead of actual respondents
  • Used when direct contact is not possible or respondents are unwilling
  • Requires careful cross-checking of information
Example

Collecting information about a factory accident from workers and supervisors.

3Ô∏è‚É£ Information from Local Sources or Correspondents

  • Local agents or correspondents are appointed in different areas
  • They regularly supply information to the investigator
  • Commonly used by newspapers, government agencies, and research organizations
Example

Newspapers collecting regional price data through local reporters.

4Ô∏è‚É£ Information Through Questionnaires and Schedules

Data are collected using pre-designed questions.

üîπ Questionnaire
  • Sent to respondents (email/post/online form)
  • Respondents fill it themselves
  • Cheaper but response rate may be low
üîπ Schedule
  • Filled by an enumerator during face-to-face interviews
  • Higher accuracy and response rate
  • Used in census surveys

Secondary Data Collection Methods

Secondary data are data that have already been collected and processed by someone else for a purpose different from the current study. These data are reused by the investigator for analysis.

üîµ Sources of Secondary Data

üìö 1Ô∏è‚É£ Published Sources

Data that are publicly available in printed or digital form.

Common Sources:

  • Government reports (economic surveys, statistical abstracts)
  • Census data
  • Research journals and publications
  • Newspapers and magazines
  • Company annual reports

‚úÖ Advantages: Easily available, Low cost, Time-saving

‚ö†Ô∏è Limitations: May not perfectly suit objective, Data may be outdated

üíæ 2Ô∏è‚É£ Unpublished Sources

Data not publicly available, but maintained by institutions or organizations.

Common Sources:

  • Internal company records
  • University project and thesis reports
  • Hospital and medical records
  • Office files and private documents

‚úÖ Advantages: More detailed, Often more specific

‚ö†Ô∏è Limitations: Difficult to access, Confidentiality issues

üìä Examples of Secondary Data Use

  • Using World Bank population statistics for demographic analysis
  • Using previous sales records of a company to forecast future demand

üßæ Summary Table: Sources of Secondary Data

Basis Published Sources Unpublished Sources

Difference Between Primary Data and Secondary Data

Basis Primary Data Secondary Data
Meaning Data originally collected by the investigator for the first time Data that already exist and were collected earlier
Originality Original in nature, collected directly from source Not original, already in existence
Suitability Always collected for specific objective Collected earlier for different objective
Cost Costly in terms of time, money, and effort Less expensive and economical
Reliability More reliable (collected firsthand) Less reliable (depends on original source)

  5. Analyze: mean, median, performance trends

Types of Scales (Nominal, Ordinal, Interval, Ratio)

What are Scales of Measurement?

Scales of measurement define how data is measured, categorized, and analyzed in statistics.

üëâ In simple terms:

They tell us what mathematical operations are allowed on data.

Example: Can we count? Can we rank? Can we add/subtract? Can we multiply/divide?

Why is it Important?

üìò Statistics:

  • Determines which statistical measures to use (mean, median, mode)
  • Determines which graphs are valid
  • Prevents wrong analysis

üíª Data Science & AI/ML:

  • Feature selection depends on scale type
  • Encoding techniques differ (One-hot encoding, Label encoding)
  • Wrong scale ‚Üí wrong model behavior

üíº Business & Research:

  • Survey design
  • Market research
  • Psychometric & social science studies

üîë Key Terms

Variable A characteristic that can change (age, gender, marks)
Measurement Scale Rule for assigning values to variables
Categorical Data Data in categories (gender, color)
Numerical Data Data in numbers (age, income)

üìç Nominal Scale (1st Level of Measurement)

Meaning
  • Classifies data into categories or names only
  • No order or ranking is possible

Key Features

  • Only classification
  • No numerical meaning
  • No comparison of magnitude

Examples

  • Gender (Male, Female)
  • Blood Group (A, B, AB, O)
  • Nationality
  • Marital Status

Statistical Use

  • Mode
  • Frequency tables
  • Bar charts

üìä Ordinal Scale (2nd Level of Measurement)

Definition

Data can be ranked or ordered, but the difference between ranks is not measurable.

üëâ Ordinal = Order matters, gap doesn't

Characteristics

  • Relative position matters
  • No equal intervals

Examples

  • Student ranks (1st, 2nd, 3rd)
  • Satisfaction levels (Poor, Average, Good, Excellent)
  • Education level (Primary, Secondary, Graduate)

Real-Life Example

Ranks in an Exam
  • Aman ‚Äì 1st
  • Anjali ‚Äì 2nd
  • Arindam ‚Äì 3rd

We know who is better, not how much better.

Allowed Operations

‚úî Allowed:

  • Comparison
  • Median
  • Percentiles

‚ùå Not Allowed:

  • Mean
  • Arithmetic operations

üìè Interval Scale (3rd Level of Measurement)

Definition

Ordered data with equal intervals, but no true zero.

üëâ Interval = Order + Equal gaps, zero is arbitrary

Characteristics

  • Differences are meaningful
  • Ratios are meaningless

Examples

  • Temperature (¬∞C, ¬∞F)
  • Calendar years
  • IQ scores

Real-Life Example

Temperatures:

Allowed Operations

‚úî Allowed:

  • Mean
  • Median
  • Standard deviation
  • Addition & subtraction

‚ùå Not Allowed:

  • Multiplication
  • Division

üìê Ratio Scale (4th Level of Measurement)

Definition

Interval scale with a true zero. Ratios are meaningful.

üëâ Ratio = Complete scale

Characteristics

  • All mathematical operations allowed
  • Zero means absence of quantity

Examples

  • Height
  • Weight
  • Age
  • Income
  • Distance

Real-Life Example

  • ‚Çπ0 ‚Üí No sales

  • Median
  • Mode

Frequency Distribution

What is Frequency Distribution?

A frequency distribution is a systematic arrangement of data that shows how often (frequency) each value or group of values occurs.

üëâ In simple words:

It tells us how data is spread.

Instead of looking at a long list of numbers, frequency distribution summarizes data neatly.

üîë Key Terms

Frequency (f) Number of times a value occurs
Class Interval Group or range of values (Example: 0‚Äì10, 10‚Äì20)
Class Limits Lower limit and Upper limit
Class Width Upper limit ‚àí Lower limit
Mid-point (Class Mark) (Lower limit + Upper limit) / 2

Types of Frequency Distribution

1Ô∏è‚É£ Ungrouped Frequency Distribution
2Ô∏è‚É£ Grouped Frequency Distribution

  • Suitable for small datasets

üëâ Easy to understand, but not suitable for large data

Grouped Frequency Distribution

Definition
  • Data grouped into class intervals
  • Used for large datasets

ü™ú Steps to Construct Grouped Frequency Distribution

  1. Find Range: Range = Max value ‚àí Min value
  2. Decide Number of Classes: Usually 5 to 15 classes
  3. Calculate Class Width: Class Width = Range / Number of Classes
  4. Form Class Intervals
  5. Count Frequencies

Tabulation and Classification

What are Classification and Tabulation?

Classification

The process of grouping raw data into meaningful categories or classes based on common characteristics.

Tabulation

The process of presenting classified data in a systematic table format using rows and columns.

üëâ Classification organizes data ‚Üí Tabulation presents it clearly

üîë Key Terms

Raw Data Data in its original unorganized form
Classification Grouping data into classes
Tabulation Presentation of classified data in tables
Class A group/category of data
Attributes Qualitative characteristics (gender, category)
Variables Quantitative characteristics (age, income)

Classification of Data

Meaning: Classification arranges data into homogeneous groups so that:

  • Similar items are together
  • Dissimilar items are separated

Types of Classification

1Ô∏è‚É£

Qualitative Classification

Based on qualities or attributes (non-numerical)

Examples: Gender, Employment status, Literacy

2Ô∏è‚É£

Quantitative Classification

Based on numerical values (forms class intervals)

Examples: Age groups, Income groups, Marks ranges

3Ô∏è‚É£

Chronological Classification

Data arranged according to time

Examples: Sales by year, Population growth by decade

4Ô∏è‚É£

Geographical Classification

Data classified by location

Examples: State-wise population, Country-wise GDP
