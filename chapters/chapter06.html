Skewness: Definition and Types

What is Skewness?

Skewness is a statistical measure that describes the asymmetry of a data distribution around its central value (mean/median).

In simple words, skewness tells us:

Is the data leaning more towards the left or the right?

A perfectly balanced (symmetric) distribution has zero skewness.

Why is Skewness Important?

üìò In Statistics:

  • Helps understand the shape of distribution
  • Affects interpretation of mean, median, and mode
  • Common in theory, numericals, and MCQs

üìä In Data Science:

  • Indicates data imbalance
  • Guides choice of transformation techniques
  • Helps in proper EDA

ü§ñ In AI / Machine Learning:

  • Skewed features can degrade model performance
  • Impacts normalization & standardization
  • Influences algorithm assumptions (e.g., linear models)

Core Concepts & Definitions

üîπ Distribution

  • Arrangement of data values across a range
  • Can be symmetric or asymmetric

üîπ Symmetry

  • Left side ‚âà Right side around the center
  • Mean = Median = Mode

üîπ Skewness

  • Degree and direction of asymmetry
  • Indicates tail direction, not data concentration

üìå Important Rule to Remember

üëâ Skewness is determined by the direction of the longer tail

Types of Skewness

Types of Skewness Visualization

üîπ 1. Zero Skewness (Symmetrical Distribution)

  • Distribution is perfectly balanced
  • Left tail = Right tail
  • Mean = Median = Mode
üìå Examples
  • Heights of students
  • Measurement errors

üîπ 2. Positive Skewness (Right-Skewed Distribution)

  • Long tail on the right side
  • Most data values are small
  • Few extremely large values
üìê Relationship:
Mean > Median > Mode
üìå Examples
  • Income distribution
  • House prices
  • Exam marks (hard paper)

üîπ 3. Negative Skewness (Left-Skewed Distribution)

  • Long tail on the left side
  • Most data values are large
  • Few extremely small values
üìê Relationship:
Mean < Median < Mode
üìå Examples
  • Easy exam scores
  • Employee age near retirement

Real-Life / Practical Examples

üéì Student Marks
  • Easy exam ‚Üí Negative skewness
  • Moderate exam ‚Üí Symmetric
  • Very hard exam ‚Üí Positive skewness
üè™ Income Distribution
  • Majority earn moderate income
  • Few earn extremely high income
  • Result ‚Üí Positive skewness

Mathematical / Numerical Explanation (Intuitive)

üîπ Mean‚ÄìMedian‚ÄìMode Rule

Type of Skewness Relationship
Symmetric Mean = Median = Mode
Positive (Right) Mean > Median > Mode
Negative (Left) Mean < Median < Mode
üìå This rule is very important for exams.

Measuring Peakedness: Kurtosis

What is Kurtosis?

Kurtosis is a statistical measure that describes the peakedness or flatness of a data distribution relative to a normal distribution.

In simple terms, kurtosis answers:

How sharp is the peak and how heavy are the tails of the distribution?

üìå Kurtosis is about tails + peak, not skewness (which is about symmetry).

Why is Kurtosis Important?

üìò In Statistics:

  • Describes shape of distribution
  • Helps compare distributions with same mean & variance
  • Common in theory and MCQs

üìä In Data Science:

  • Detects extreme values (heavy tails)
  • Important in risk and anomaly analysis
  • Improves understanding of feature behavior

ü§ñ In AI / Machine Learning:

  • Heavy-tailed features can affect model stability
  • Helps in choosing robust models
  • Useful in financial & sensor data

Core Concepts & Definitions

üîπ Peakedness

  • Degree to which data is concentrated around the mean
  • Sharp peak ‚Üí more concentration
  • Flat peak ‚Üí less concentration

üîπ Tails

  • Extreme ends of the distribution
  • Heavy tails ‚Üí more extreme values
  • Light tails ‚Üí fewer extreme values

üîπ Kurtosis (Mathematical Meaning)

  • Based on the fourth central moment
  • Measures tail heaviness more than just peak height

Types of Kurtosis

üìä

1. Mesokurtic (Normal)

  • Moderate peak
  • Moderate tails
  • Kurtosis ‚âà 3 (raw kurtosis)
üìå Example: Normal distribution
üìà

2. Leptokurtic (High Peak)

  • Sharp peak
  • Heavy tails
  • More extreme values
  • Kurtosis > 3
üìå Examples: Financial returns, Network traffic data
üìâ

3. Platykurtic (Flat Peak)

  • Flat or broad peak
  • Thin tails
  • Fewer outliers
  • Kurtosis < 3
üìå Example: Uniform-like distributions

Real-Life / Practical Examples

üè¶ Finance (Stock Returns)

üéì Exam Scores

Interpretation Using Graphs and Formulas

What does "Interpretation using Graphs and Formulas" mean?

This topic focuses on reading, understanding, and concluding about a dataset by using:

  • üìà Graphs (histogram, density curve, boxplot)
  • üßÆ Formulas & numerical values (skewness, kurtosis)

In practice, data scientists and statisticians rarely stop at calculation‚Äîthey must interpret what the numbers and shapes mean.

Why This Topic is Important

üìò In Statistics:

  • Converts formulas into meaningful conclusions
  • Essential for descriptive analysis
  • Very common in theory + interpretation questions

üìä In Data Science:

  • Core part of Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)
  • Helps choose transformations
  • Guides model assumptions

ü§ñ In AI / Machine Learning:

  • Identifies skewed or heavy-tailed features
  • Improves preprocessing decisions
  • Affects model accuracy & stability

Core Concepts & Definitions

üîπ Graphical Interpretation

Understanding distribution shape using:

  • Histogram
  • Density curve
  • Boxplot

üîπ Formula-Based Interpretation

Using numerical measures:

  • Skewness ‚Üí asymmetry
  • Kurtosis ‚Üí peakedness & tail heaviness

üîπ Key Rule (Very Important)

Always interpret graphs first, formulas second.

Formulas confirm what graphs visually suggest.
