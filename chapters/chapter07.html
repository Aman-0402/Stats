Pearson's Correlation Coefficient (Karl Pearson's r)

What is Pearson's Correlation?

Pearson's correlation coefficient (denoted by r) measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two numerical (quantitative) variables.

In simple words, it answers:

When X changes, how strongly and in which direction does Y change?

Why is Pearson's Correlation Important?

It is important in fields like data science, finance, healthcare, and social sciences, where understanding relationships between different factors is important. Quantifying the degree to which two variables are related helps analysts, researchers, and decision-makers to find patterns that make predictions and inform decisions.

Visualizing the Pearson Correlation Coefficient

Another way to think of the Pearson correlation coefficient (r) is as a measure of how close the observations are to a line of best fit.

The Pearson correlation coefficient also tells you whether the slope of the line of best fit is negative or positive. When the slope is negative, r is negative. When the slope is positive, r is positive.

Perfect Correlation (r = +1 or r = ‚àí1)

When r is 1 or ‚Äì1, all the points fall exactly on the line of best fit:

Strong Correlation (r > .5 or r < ‚àí.5)

When r is greater than .5 or less than ‚Äì.5, the points are close to the line of best fit:

Weak Correlation (0 < r < .3 or ‚àí.3 < r < 0)

When r is between 0 and .3 or between 0 and ‚Äì.3, the points are far from the line of best fit:

Zero Correlation (r = 0)

Spearman's Rank Correlation Coefficient (œÅ / rs)

What is Spearman's Rank Correlation?

Spearman's Rank Correlation Coefficient is a non-parametric measure that assesses the strength and direction of a monotonic relationship between two variables, using their ranks instead of actual values.

In simple words:

It measures how well the relationship between two variables can be described using ranks

üìå It is denoted by œÅ (rho) or rs

Why is Spearman's Correlation Important?

üìò In Statistics:

  • Used when data is ordinal (ranked)
  • Useful when Pearson's assumptions fail
  • Very common in exam numericals

üìä In Data Science:

  • Works well with nonlinear but monotonic relationships
  • Robust to outliers
  • Suitable for survey & ranking data

ü§ñ In AI / Machine Learning:

  • Feature selection when relationships are monotonic
  • Useful for non-normal data
  • More reliable when data contains extreme values

Core Concepts & Definitions

üîπ Rank

  • Position of a value when data is arranged in ascending or descending order
  • Smallest value ‚Üí Rank 1 (usually)

üîπ Monotonic Relationship

Variables move in one direction only:

  • Increase‚Äìincrease
  • Decrease‚Äìdecrease
  • Not necessarily linear

üîπ Spearman's Correlation Coefficient (œÅ)

Value ranges from ‚àí1 to +1

Value Interpretation
+1 Perfect positive rank correlation
‚àí1 Perfect negative rank correlation
0 No rank correlation

Real-Life / Practical Examples

üéì Example 1: Student Ranks in Two Subjects
  • Compare ranks in Math vs Physics
  • Actual marks don't matter, relative position matters
üè™ Example 2: Product Popularity Ranking
  • Online product ratings
  • Customer preference ranks

When to Prefer Spearman Over Pearson

Situation Preferred Method
Data is ranked Spearman
Outliers present Spearman
Nonlinear but monotonic Spearman

Scatter Plots

What is a Scatter Plot?

A scatter plot is a graphical representation that shows the relationship between two numerical variables by plotting data points on a 2D coordinate plane.

Each point represents one observation:

  • X-axis ‚Üí Independent variable
  • Y-axis ‚Üí Dependent variable
üìå Scatter plots are the foundation of correlation and regression analysis

Why are Scatter Plots Important?

üìò In Statistics:

  • Visualizes association between variables
  • Helps detect patterns, trends, and outliers
  • First step before calculating correlation

üìä In Data Science:

  • Core tool in Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)
  • Understands feature‚Äìtarget relationships
  • Checks linearity assumption

ü§ñ In AI / Machine Learning:

  • Feature selection
  • Detecting nonlinear relationships
  • Diagnosing model assumptions

Core Concepts & Definitions

üîπ Data Point

  • One pair of values (x, y)
  • Represents one observation

üîπ Trend

Overall direction of points:

  • Upward
  • Downward
  • No clear direction

üîπ Types of Relationships

  • Positive relationship
  • Negative relationship
  • No relationship
  • Nonlinear relationship

üîπ Outliers

  • Points far away from the main cluster
  • Can heavily affect correlation & regression

Real-Life / Practical Examples

üéì Example 1: Study Hours vs Marks
  • More study ‚Üí better marks
  • Positive relationship
üí∞ Example 2: Price vs Demand
  • Higher price ‚Üí lower demand
  • Negative relationship
üå¶ Example 3: Shoe Size vs Intelligence
  • No meaningful relationship
  • No correlation

Mathematical / Conceptual Interpretation

Scatter plots help answer:

  • Do variables move together?
  • Is the relationship linear or nonlinear?
  • Are there outliers?
  • Is correlation meaningful?

üìå Important:

Scatter plot ‚Üí visual insight

Correlation coefficient ‚Üí numerical confirmation

Scatter Plot Patterns (Very Important)
