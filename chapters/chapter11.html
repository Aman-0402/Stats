Part 1: Types of Sampling

üîπ What is Sampling?

Sampling is the process of selecting a subset (sample) from a population to draw conclusions about the whole population.

üëâ Why sampling?

  • Population may be too large
  • Saves time, cost, effort
  • Used everywhere in Statistics, ML, surveys

üß† Population vs Sample (Quick Recall)

  • Population ‚Üí Entire group (all students, all voters)
  • Sample ‚Üí Selected subset (100 students from college)

üîπ Types of Sampling

We'll cover the most important ones used in exams and data science.

1Ô∏è‚É£ Simple Random Sampling (SRS)

‚úÖ Definition

Every individual in the population has an equal chance of being selected

üéì Example

From 1,000 students, randomly select 100 students using a lottery or random number generator.

‚úîÔ∏è Fair and unbiased

üß™ Python Example

üëç Advantages

  • Easy to understand
  • No bias (if truly random)

üëé Disadvantages

  • Not practical for very large populations

2Ô∏è‚É£ Stratified Sampling

‚úÖ Definition

Population is divided into homogeneous groups (strata), then random samples are taken from each stratum

üéì Example

College population:

  • 60% boys
  • 40% girls

Sample of 100:

  • 60 boys
  • 40 girls

‚úîÔ∏è Ensures proper representation

üß† When to Use?

When population has subgroups:

  • Gender, department, income groups, age groups

üëç Advantages

  • More representative
  • Higher accuracy

üëé Disadvantages

  • Needs prior population information

3Ô∏è‚É£ Systematic Sampling

‚úÖ Definition

Select every k-th element from the population after a random start

k = N / n
üéì Example

Population = 1,000
Sample size = 100

k = 1000 / 100 = 10

Pick every 10th person

üëç Advantages

  • Simple and fast
  • Easy to implement

üëé Disadvantages

  • Can be biased if pattern exists

4Ô∏è‚É£ Cluster Sampling

‚úÖ Definition

Population is divided into clusters, then entire clusters are randomly selected

üéì Example

A city has 100 schools.

Randomly select 10 schools and survey all students in them.

‚úîÔ∏è Cost-effective for large areas

üëç Advantages

  • Cheap
  • Suitable for large geographic areas

üëé Disadvantages

  • Less accurate than stratified sampling

5Ô∏è‚É£ Convenience Sampling (Non-Probability)

‚úÖ Definition

Sample is chosen based on ease of access

üéì Example

Survey students sitting in the front row only.

‚ö†Ô∏è Highly biased ‚Üí not preferred in exams

Part 2: Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

What is the Central Limit Theorem?

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) says:

If we take sufficiently large random samples from any population, the distribution of the sample mean will be approximately normal, regardless of the population's original distribution.

üìå This is true even if the population is skewed or non-normal

üß† Why CLT is So Important?

Because it:

  • Justifies using Normal Distribution everywhere
  • Powers confidence intervals
  • Enables hypothesis testing
  • Is the foundation of inferential statistics
  • Explains why many ML errors look normal

üëâ Without CLT, most statistics would collapse

üîë Key Conditions for CLT

  1. Samples must be random
  2. Sample size should be large
    • Rule of thumb: n ‚â• 30
  3. Observations must be independent
‚ö†Ô∏è Population does NOT need to be normal

Part 3: Standard Error

üîπ What is Standard Error?

Standard Error (SE) measures the variability of a sample statistic (usually the sample mean) from sample to sample.

üëâ In simple words:

Standard deviation of the sampling distribution

üß† Why Standard Error is Important?

Because it tells us:

  • How precise our sample mean is
  • How close sample mean is to population mean
  • Basis for:
    • Confidence intervals
    • Hypothesis testing
    • Z-tests & t-tests
